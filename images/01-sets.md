# Figure briefs — Chapter 1: Sets

## Figure 1: Two-set Venn diagram (general)

**Placement:** Concept 3, immediately after the paragraph introducing Venn diagrams.

**Description:** Rectangle labeled "U" (universal set). Two overlapping circles inside it, labeled "A" and "B". The four resulting regions are: A only (in A but not B), B only (in B but not A), A ∩ B (the lens-shaped overlap), and (A ∪ B)' (the rectangle's interior outside both circles).

**Pedagogical purpose:** The student should immediately see the four regions and recognize that any two intersecting sets partition the universe into exactly four cells. This is the visual base for every two-set cardinality argument later in the chapter.

**Style notes:** Schematic, not photorealistic. Circles with thin black outlines on white. Light fill (e.g., pale blue) for circle A, light fill (e.g., pale yellow) for circle B; overlap blended (pale green). Region labels in small italic text inside each cell. The rectangle label "U" sits in the top-right corner.

## Figure 2: Three-set Venn diagram (general)

**Placement:** Concept 3, immediately after Figure 1.

**Description:** Rectangle labeled "U". Three overlapping circles inside it, labeled "A", "B", and "C", arranged so that all three pairwise overlaps exist plus a central three-way intersection $A \cap B \cap C$. Total: eight regions (seven inside the circles plus one outside).

**Pedagogical purpose:** The student should count all eight regions and recognize that adding a third set roughly doubles the diagram's complexity. This is the visual base for the three-set worked examples (study survey, blood bank).

**Style notes:** Schematic. Three colors for the three circles (pale blue, pale yellow, pale red) blending in their overlaps to give six distinguishable shades plus the central darker region. Region labels: small italic text reading $A \cap B' \cap C'$, $A' \cap B \cap C'$, etc.

## Figure 3: Three-set Venn diagram populated with blood-donor cardinalities

**Placement:** Integration section, illustrating the 100-donor blood-bank example.

**Description:** Same three-circle layout as Figure 2, with circles labeled "A", "B", and "Rh+". Each of the eight regions populated with a count and a blood-type label:

| Region | Label | Count |
|---|---|---|
| Outside all three | O− | 7 |
| Only A | A− | 6 |
| Only B | B− | 2 |
| Only Rh+ | O+ | 12 |
| A and Rh+ only | A+ | 36 |
| B and Rh+ only | B+ | 33 |
| A and B only | AB− | 1 |
| All three | AB+ | 3 |

**Pedagogical purpose:** The student should be able to recover any union, intersection, or complement count by adding the appropriate regions. The total of the eight regions sums to 100, the universe.

**Style notes:** Schematic, same color conventions as Figure 2. Numbers in bold black; blood-type labels in small italic above each number. Title: "100 donors at the Saturday blood drive." Caption beneath: "Adding any single circle's regions gives the cardinality of that blood factor; the eight regions together sum to 100."

## Figure 4 (optional): Galileo's pairing of naturals with squares

**Placement:** Concept 2, after the paragraph introducing equivalent infinite subsets.

**Description:** Two parallel rows. Top row: $1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7, 8, \ldots$ Bottom row: $1, 4, 9, 16, 25, 36, 49, 64, \ldots$ Vertical arrows connect each top-row natural number to its square below.

**Pedagogical purpose:** The student should see, visually, that every natural pairs uniquely with a square — and conclude (as Galileo did) that the two infinite sets are equivalent in size, even though squares are scarcer.

**Style notes:** Simple, schematic. Numbers in serif font, arrows thin. Caption: "Every natural number pairs with one square. Every square pairs with one natural number. Same cardinality, even though one set sits inside the other."
