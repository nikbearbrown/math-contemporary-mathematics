# Images — Chapter 3: Real Numbers and Number Theory

## [FIGURE: Factor tree for 140]
A tree diagram with 140 at the root. First split into 2 and 70. The 2 is marked as a leaf (prime). The 70 splits into 2 and 35. The second 2 is marked as a leaf. The 35 splits into 5 and 7. Both 5 and 7 are marked as leaves (prime). The leaves read left-to-right: 2, 2, 5, 7. Caption: "Prime factorization of 140: $2^2 \times 5 \times 7$."

## [FIGURE: Number line with integers and absolute value]
A horizontal line with marks at ..., −3, −2, −1, 0, 1, 2, 3, ... . An arc above the line between 0 and 5 is labeled "5 units" with an arrow pointing to 5. An arc below the line between 0 and −5 is labeled "5 units" with an arrow pointing to −5. Caption: "Both 5 and −5 are 5 units from 0. Absolute values: $|5| = 5$ and $|-5| = 5$."

## [FIGURE: Addition on a number line]
A number line with marks at 0, 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7, 8. An arrow starting at 3, pointing right, labeled "+5". The arrow ends at 8. Caption: "$3 + 5 = 8$. Start at 3, move right 5 units."

## [FIGURE: Subtraction of a negative on a number line]
A number line with marks at −3, −2, −1, 0, 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7. An arrow starting at 7, pointing left, labeled "−(−3)" or "add 3". The arrow ends at 10. Caption: "$7 − (−3) = 10$. Subtracting a negative flips direction: move left becomes move right."

## [FIGURE: Pizza slices as fractions]
A circle divided into 8 equal slices. 3 slices are shaded. Caption: "A pizza cut into 8 equal pieces. You have 3 pieces. That's $\frac{3}{8}$ of the pizza."

## [FIGURE: Equivalent fractions visually]
Two rectangles, same size. First rectangle divided into 5 equal parts, 3 shaded. Label: "$\frac{3}{5}$". Second rectangle divided into 10 equal parts, 6 shaded. Label: "$\frac{6}{10}$". Both shaded regions visually cover the same area. Caption: "$\frac{3}{5}$ and $\frac{6}{10}$ are equivalent. Both represent 60% of the whole."

## [FIGURE: Common denominator visualization]
Two rectangles, same size, stacked. Top: divided into 4 parts, 1 shaded, labeled "$\frac{1}{4}$". Bottom: divided into 6 parts, 1 shaded, labeled "$\frac{1}{6}$". Below: a single rectangle divided into 12 parts. Top section (first 3 parts): shaded lightly, labeled "$\frac{3}{12}$" (from $\frac{1}{4}$). Next section (next 2 parts): shaded darker, labeled "$\frac{2}{12}$" (from $\frac{1}{6}$). Total shaded (5 parts): labeled "$\frac{5}{12}$" (the sum). Caption: "$\frac{1}{4} + \frac{1}{6}$: Find common denominator 12. Convert $\frac{1}{4} = \frac{3}{12}$ and $\frac{1}{6} = \frac{2}{12}$. Add: $\frac{3}{12} + \frac{2}{12} = \frac{5}{12}$."

## [FIGURE: GCD and LCM on a Venn diagram]
A Venn diagram with two overlapping circles. Left circle: prime factors of 12 = {2, 2, 3}. Right circle: prime factors of 18 = {2, 3, 3}. Overlapping region: {2, 3} (the common prime factors). Outside overlap (left): {2} (extra factor in 12). Outside overlap (right): {3} (extra factor in 18). GCD (overlap only): 2 × 3 = 6. LCM (all factors): 2 × 3 × 2 × 3 = 36. Caption: "GCD uses only the common prime factors. LCM uses each prime factor the maximum number of times it appears."

## [FIGURE: Bank account as integer movement]
A timeline showing: Start: $0. After deposit of $50: +$50. After withdrawal of $30: +$20 (50 − 30). After overdraft of $25: −$5 (20 − 25). Each transition is labeled with the operation. Caption: "Integer operations model money. Positive = deposits, negative = debits. The rules don't change."

## [FIGURE: Prime factorization uniqueness]
Three rows. Row 1: "72 = 2 × 36 = 2 × 4 × 9 = 2 × 2 × 2 × 3 × 3." Row 2: "Different ways to find the factorization, but one end result." Row 3: "Prime factorization: $2^3 \times 3^2$." Caption: "All roads lead to the same prime factorization. That uniqueness is the Fundamental Theorem of Arithmetic."
