**Caption:** A property of the golden rectangle: when you cut off a square from a golden rectangle, the remainder is also a golden rectangle. This self-similarity allows infinite nesting.

### Figure 2b: Frequency spectrum diagram
**Description:** A horizontal bar showing the frequency spectrum from 0 to 20,000 Hz. Mark regions: infrasound (below 20 Hz), human hearing range (20 Hz to 20,000 Hz), ultrasound (above 20,000 Hz). Annotate specific frequencies: 20 Hz (low rumble), 262 Hz (Middle C), 440 Hz (A4, standard pitch), 20,000 Hz (high whistle), 40,000 Hz (dog whistle).

**Source:** Generated graphic.

**Caption:** The human hearing range spans approximately 20 Hz to 20,000 Hz. Sounds below are infrasonic; above are ultrasonic. Musical frequencies span a much narrower range, from about 27.5 Hz (lowest note on a 88-key piano) to 4,186 Hz (highest note).

### Figure 2c: Sine wave interference
**Description:** Two sine waves at different frequencies (e.g., C at 262 Hz and E at 330 Hz) drawn one above the other, then their sum shown below. Highlight regions where the waves are in phase (constructive interference, louder) and out of phase (destructive interference, quieter).

**Source:** Generated graphic using audio software or graphing tool.

**Caption:** When two notes sound together, their sound waves interfere. If the frequencies are in a simple ratio (e.g., 3:2 for a perfect fifth), the interference pattern repeats regularly and the ear perceives consonance. Complex ratios create an unstable, dissonant effect.

---

### Figure 2d: Equal temperament frequency multipliers
**Description:** A bar chart or table showing the frequency multiplier for each half-step in an octave. Start with 262 Hz (C4). Show that each half-step multiplies by $2^{1/12} \approx 1.0595$. Mark key intervals: octave ($2^{1/1} = 2$), fifth ($2^{7/12} \approx 1.5$), fourth ($2^{5/12} \approx 1.33$).

**Source:** Generated table or graphic.

**Caption:** Equal-temperament tuning divides an octave into 12 equal steps. Each step multiplies frequency by $2^{1/12}$, ensuring that transposed melodies sound identical. This irrational multiplier emerges from the constraint that all octaves be uniform.

## Notes on sourcing

- **Photographs of natural forms** (sunflowers, shells, galaxies) can be sourced from stock photography (Unsplash, Pexels) or high-quality public domain sources (NASA, Wikipedia Commons).
- **Artwork** (da Vinci, Escher) is largely in the public domain or available under fair use for educational purposes.
- **Diagrams** (sine waves, tournament brackets, historical curves) should be generated fresh or sourced from educational open-access resources.
- **Golden rectangle overlays** should be added to existing images to avoid copyright issues; the overlay is a teaching tool.

All images should be accompanied by captions that explain what the viewer should notice and why it relates to the mathematical concept.
