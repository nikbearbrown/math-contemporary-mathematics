# Images manifest — Chapter 8: Statistics

## Images preserved from source

These images should be created or sourced to support the three core concepts:

### Sampling and bias

**[FIGURE: Literary Digest sampling error, 1936]** Timeline or cartoon showing how the 1936 Literary Digest poll went wrong. Two overlapping distributions: the sample (wealthier, skewed Republican) and the actual voting population (more economically diverse, leaned Roosevelt). Illustrates how sample composition matters more than sample size.

**[FIGURE: Sampling method comparison]** Four small diagrams illustrating simple random, systematic, stratified, and cluster sampling on a hypothetical population grid. Shows which methods are likely to capture the full diversity of the population.

### Measures of center and spread

**[FIGURE: Mean vs. median under skew]** A histogram that is right-skewed (bunched on the left, tail extending right). Vertical lines show the mean (pulled rightward by the tail) and the median (centered in the bulk of the data). Caption explains why median is more robust to outliers.

**[FIGURE: Effect of standard deviation]** Two bell curves with the same mean but different standard deviations (one narrow/peaked, one wide/flat). Side-by-side, showing how standard deviation controls the width of the distribution.

**[FIGURE: WNBA salary example]** A histogram or dot plot of WNBA salaries, marked with the mean, median, and mode. Shows how Candace Parker's salary pulls the mean upward but leaves the median near the bulk of the data.

### Normal distribution and confidence

**[FIGURE: The 68-95-99.7 rule]** A bell curve with three shaded bands: ±1 SD (68%), ±2 SD (95%), ±3 SD (99.7%). Numbers and percentages clearly labeled. This is a canonical image; use or adapt from a textbook figure.

**[FIGURE: John Kerrich's coin-flip experiment simulation]** Four histograms showing convergence to the normal distribution as the number of trials increases (100 trials, 100 trials repeated 100 times, then 1,000). Shows how randomness at scale produces shape.

**[FIGURE: Z-score comparison across populations]** Two normal curves (one for SAT, one for ACT, with different means and standard deviations). A point marked on each curve at the same z-score, visually showing how z-scores allow comparison across different distributions.

**[FIGURE: Percentile bands]** A bell curve with percentile labels: 50th at the mean, 84th at one standard deviation above, 16th at one standard deviation below, etc. Shows the percentile-to-z correspondence visually.

## Data visualization principles

The chapter deliberately reduces visual complexity (this is not a data visualization chapter; it's a statistics chapter). Graphics should:
- Use clear, single-purpose illustrations.
- Avoid decorative elements (no 3D, no icons substituting for bars, no color gradients that don't carry meaning).
- Label axes and percentages explicitly.
- Include brief captions that name what the illustration shows and why it matters.

## Not included (deferred to future chapters)

- Detailed bar charts and pie charts (visual design Chapter 8 material, not core statistics).
- Scatter plots and regression lines (Chapter 9+ material).
- Detailed histograms of real datasets (SAT scores, tuition data, etc. from source are too heavy for a rewrite focused on core concepts).
