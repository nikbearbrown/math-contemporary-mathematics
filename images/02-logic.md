# Figure briefs — Chapter 2: Logic

## Figure 1: Truth table for negation
**Placement:** Concept 1, after "The negation of a statement reverses its truth value."

**Description:** A simple two-row, two-column table showing $p$ (true, false) and $\sim p$ (false, true). Visually, a flip or mirror.

**Pedagogical purpose:** Students should notice that the second column is the opposite of the first — negation is mechanical, not semantic.

**Style notes:** Plain text table, no color. Headers: $p$ and $\sim p$.

## Figure 2: Truth table for AND (conjunction) and OR (disjunction)
**Placement:** Concept 2, in the section "Building a truth table for AND and OR."

**Description:** A four-row truth table with columns: $p$, $q$, $p \land q$, $p \lor q$. Rows show all four combinations (TT, TF, FT, FF).

**Pedagogical purpose:** Students should see at a glance:
- AND is true only when both parts are true (row 1 only).
- OR is false only when both parts are false (row 4 only).
- The contrast between the two connectives is visible in the columns.

**Style notes:** Plain text table. Optional: light gray background on the all-false row to highlight the "corner case" for OR.

---

## Figure 3: Truth table for conditional (p → q)
**Placement:** Concept 3, in the section "Valid logical arguments."

**Description:** A four-row truth table with columns: $p$, $q$, $p \rightarrow q$. Highlight row 2 (T, F, F) to show the only false case.

**Pedagogical purpose:** Students should notice that the conditional is true in three out of four cases. Specifically, the conditional is false ONLY when the hypothesis is true and the conclusion is false. This is the key to understanding why the law of detachment is valid.

**Style notes:** Plain text table. Optional: bold or color the row where the conditional is false.

---

## Figure 4: Venn diagram for law of detachment
**Placement:** Not in chapter but referenced in "Concept 3" discussion; could be optional.

**Description:** Two nested circles: guitarists (inside) and musicians (outside). A point × marked inside the guitarists' circle, with the label "p is true." Arrows pointing to the fact that × is therefore inside the musicians' circle (q is true).

**Pedagogical purpose:** Students should see that the law of detachment is valid because premise 1 ($p \rightarrow q$: guitarists are a subset of musicians) combined with premise 2 ($p$: this person is a guitarist) forces the conclusion ($q$: this person is a musician).

**Style notes:** Labeled circles, clear legend. Not essential for the chapter (truth tables suffice), but helpful for visual learners.

---

## Note on scope

The chapter focuses on truth tables as the primary tool for validating statements and arguments. Venn diagrams are mentioned in the source but are secondary here. If the chapter is taught with visual learners in mind, Figure 4 could be included. Otherwise, Figures 1-3 are sufficient.
