## Figures Needed (Source to Render)

### Figure 1: Simple Interest Over Time
- **Description:** A line graph showing a simple linear growth of debt or savings at 5% APR for $1,000 starting principal over 30 years.
- **X-axis:** Years (0–30)
- **Y-axis:** Dollar amount ($0–$2,500)
- **Content:** One straight line from (0, $1,000) to (30, $2,500). The line is exactly straight because interest is calculated only on the principal; no compounding.
- **Label:** "Simple Interest: $1,000 at 5% APR for 30 years. Total = $2,500. Straight line, constant slope."
- **Source file:** m00059 (simple interest section)

### Figure 2: Compound Interest Over Time (Exponential Curve)
- **Description:** A curved graph showing exponential growth of the same $1,000 at 5% APR over 30 years, compounded annually.
- **X-axis:** Years (0–30)
- **Y-axis:** Dollar amount ($0–$4,500)
- **Content:** Two curves. Line 1: simple interest (straight line to $2,500). Line 2: compound interest (exponential curve to ~$4,322). The gap between them grows with time.
- **Label:** "Simple vs. Compound Interest: $1,000 at 5% APR for 30 years. Simple (straight line) = $2,500. Compound (curved line) = $4,322. The difference accelerates with time."
- **Source file:** m00060 (compound interest section)

### Figure 3: Amortization Table Visualization
- **Description:** A stacked bar chart showing the composition of mortgage payments over time.
- **X-axis:** Payment number (1, 60, 120, 180, 240, 300, 360) for a 30-year mortgage
- **Y-axis:** Dollar amount (0–$2,000)
- **Content:** Each bar is divided into two colors: blue (principal) and red (interest). Early bars are mostly red (interest); late bars are mostly blue (principal). Same height (same payment) throughout, but composition changes.
- **Label:** "30-Year Mortgage Amortization: $300,000 at 6.5% APR. Each payment is $1,896.20. Early payments: mostly interest. Late payments: mostly principal."
- **Source file:** m00068 (amortization table section)

### Figure 4: Credit Card Trap — Balance Over Time
- **Description:** A graph showing how a credit card balance with only minimum payments decays extremely slowly (or not at all).
- **X-axis:** Months (0–360, 30 years)
- **Y-axis:** Balance owed ($0–$6,000)
- **Content:** Starting balance $5,000 at 24% APR with 2% minimum payment. The curve shows the balance barely declining for the first 20 years, then finally dropping in the last 10 years. Overlay a second line showing what the balance would be with a fixed $200 monthly payment (a much faster payoff).
- **Label:** "Credit Card Trap: $5,000 at 24% APR with minimum (2%) payment. Red line: balance with minimum payment (30+ years to payoff). Blue line: balance with fixed $200/month (much faster). The machinery favors the lender."
- **Source file:** m00066 (credit cards section)

### Figure 5: The Exponential Gap — Early Investment vs. Late Investment
- **Description:** Two curves showing how starting to save 10 years earlier dramatically changes the outcome, even with no additional contributions.
- **X-axis:** Years (0–40)
- **Y-axis:** Dollar amount ($0–$100,000)
- **Content:** Two curves. Investor A: $5,000 invested at year 0, left alone for 40 years at 7% APR = $149,745. Investor B: $5,000 invested at year 10, left alone for 30 years at 7% APR = $54,304. Same investment, different starting times, vastly different outcomes. Shade the area between the curves to show the "cost" of waiting.
- **Label:** "Time is the Most Powerful Ingredient: $5,000 invested at 7% APR. Start at year 0: $149,745 after 40 years. Start at year 10: $54,304 after 30 years. The 10-year difference: $95,441 in foregone growth."
- **Source file:** [This figure is a synthesis, not from a single source module, but illustrates compound interest principle from m00060]

## Notes on Rendering

- All figures should use a consistent color palette: blue for principal/growth, red for interest/cost.
- Figures 1, 2, and 5 are line graphs best rendered with smooth curves (not discrete points) to show the exponential nature.
- Figure 3 (amortization stacked bar) is best rendered as a stacked bar chart with clear color separation.
- Figure 4 (credit card trap) should show two lines for comparison: the predatory trap vs. the responsible payoff.
- All axes should have clear labels, tick marks, and units.
- All figures should be accessible to students without calculus background; no limit notation or formal derivative language.
