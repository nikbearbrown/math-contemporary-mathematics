## Chapter-by-Chapter Breakdown of Source Material

### Module m00035: Introduction to Metric Measurement

**What it teaches:** Metric system overview; contrast with U.S. customary system; the system is used in nearly all countries except the US, Liberia, and Myanmar.

**Key content:**
- Road trip scenario (US uses gallons/miles; Mexico uses liters/kilometers)
- Definition: metric system is a decimal measuring system using meters, liters, grams
- Statement of prevalence (all but three countries use it)

**How rewrite uses it:** The road trip scenario was replaced with the Mars Climate Orbiter crash (higher stakes, clearer puzzle, more memorable). The definitional content appears in the cold open and Concept 1.

### Module m00069: The Metric System — Units and Prefixes

**What it teaches:**
- Learning objectives: identify units, order prefixes, convert between units
- Base units: meter (m), square meter (m²), cubic meter (m³), liter (L), gram (g), degrees Celsius
- Trade-off with U.S. customary (12 inches/foot, 3 feet/yard, etc.)
- The seven common prefixes and their magnitudes
- "King Henry Died From Drinking Chocolate Milk" mnemonic
- Example: 1,200 g versus 1 kg bags (which is the better deal?)
- Worked examples: determining correct base unit, ordering magnitudes, determining reasonable values for length
- Note about historical context: 1975 Metric Conversion Act, Valerie Antoine, Reagan's dissolution of the board
- Note: Most of US already uses metric (film, ibuprofen, carb counting, sports)

**How rewrite uses it:**
- The mnemonic, prefix table, and definitions appear in Concept 1
- The worked example (pasta/masa comparison) is preserved but repurposed as Exercise 9.1
- The historical note (Valerie Antoine) is omitted per design guidelines (no career sidebars)
- The note about metric already in US life is woven into the intro
- Trade-off analysis moved to explicit "trade-off" section within Concept 1

### Module m00070: Measuring Area

**What it teaches:**
- Learning objectives: identify reasonable area values, convert area units, solve area problems
- Area formula: A = length × width
- Why conversion factors for area are exponential (1 m² = 10,000 cm²)
- Examples: paper sheet (cm²), bedroom walls (m²), football field (7,140 m²)
- Worked examples: determining reasonable units for area, determining reasonable values, explaining reasonableness
- Complex application: aluminum siding problem (account for door cutout)
- Multi-step problem: park with different length units (km and m)
- Multi-step with addition: kitchen + pantry floors, total tile needed
- Note: Origin of metric system (1780s France, 250,000 local units in use before)

**How rewrite uses it:**
- Area exponential relationship becomes core of Concept 2
- Formula and conversion table integrated into Concept 2
- Worked example (kitchen + pantry) is adapted and appears in the Integration section
- Complex application problems repurposed as Exercise 9.4
- The historical note (origin in France) is omitted

### Module m00071: Measuring Volume

**What it teaches:**
- Learning objectives: identify reasonable volume values, convert like volume units, convert different units, solve application problems
- Volume formula: V = length × width × height
- Why conversion factors for volume are cubic (1 m³ = 1,000,000 cm³)
- The liter relationship: 1 L = 1 dm³, 1 mL = 1 cm³
- Worked examples: determining reasonable volumes, explaining volumes, converting cm³ to mm³, converting m³ to cm³ (multi-step)
- Liter conversion examples: aquarium holding tank (mL to L), airplane fuel (m³ to dm³ to L)
- Complex problem: juice carton production (volume to capacity, then division to find count)
- Complex problem: fish tank (volume, percentage fill, conversion to liters)
- Exploration prompt: How does shape affect volume? (cylinder vs. box)

**How rewrite uses it:**
- Volume cubic relationship becomes core of Concept 3
- Liter relationship is anchored early and emphasized throughout
- Formula and conversion table integrated into Concept 3
- Worked example (juice carton) is adapted and appears in Concept 3
- Complex problems adapted for Exercise 9.5
- Shape exploration is omitted (beyond scope of introductory chapter)

### Module m00072: Measuring Weight (Mass)

**What it teaches:**
- Learning objectives: identify reasonable weight values, convert weight units, solve weight problems
- Distinction: mass vs. weight (mass is constant; weight is gravity-dependent)
- In metric countries, mass and weight used interchangeably
- The gram defined as mass of 1 cm³ of water
- Kilogram originally defined as mass of 1 liter of water
- Reference weights: paper clip (1 g), liter of water (1 kg), average car (2 metric tons)
- Examples: determining reasonable units (newborn: 3.5 kg vs 3.5 g), determining reasonable values (three lemons: 250 g vs 2,500 g vs 250 kg)
- Whale measurement note (drones, Fredrik Christiansen)
- Worked examples: conversion from mg to kg (multi-step), kg to g (multiply), mg to g to kg (multi-step division)
- Application: car seat safety (child weight comparison), recipe scaling (flour), complex: tomato harvest problem
- No content notes on mass-vs-weight are included

**How rewrite uses it:**
- The mass-weight distinction is briefly acknowledged but not belabored (per book audience)
- The water anchor (1 L water = 1 kg) is highlighted as central to understanding weight
- Reference weights integrated into Concept 4 and pantry benchmarks
- Worked example (pediatric dosing) adapts the car seat example
- Complex application problem (tomato harvest) adapted for Exercise 9.11
- Whale measurement note is omitted

### Module m00073: Measuring Temperature

**What it teaches:**
- Learning objectives: convert Fahrenheit and Celsius, identify reasonable temperature values, solve temperature problems
- Temperature defined as speed of atomic motion
- Celsius scale: 100 degrees between water freezing (0 °C) and boiling (100 °C)
- Common temperatures listed in both scales
- Exact conversion formulas: C = (5/9)(F − 32) and F = (9/5)C + 32
- Estimation formulas: C ≈ (F − 30)/2 and F ≈ 2C + 30
- Worked examples: convert 392°F to C (answer: 200°C), convert 20°C to F (answer: 68°F)
- Application: vaccine refrigeration (compare 3°C to required 36–46°F range)
- Examples: determining reasonable Celsius values using benchmarks (freezer, body temperature, chocolate storage)
- Complex problem: chemical compound heating (starting 20°C, must reach 392°F, can heat 9°C every 15 min — how long?)
- Note: Third temperature scale (Kelvin) exists but not emphasized in general education
- Projects: convert a recipe, compare gas prices (requiring unit conversion), research sports measurement systems

**How rewrite uses it:**
- Temperature conversion is treated as a separate topic but not as a full "Concept"
- Formulas (exact and mental-math) are placed in the pantry
- Worked examples adapted for Exercise 9.8
- Application problems adapted for Exercise 9.9
- Projects are omitted (chapter exercises replace dedicated projects)
- Kelvin mention is minimal

## Ideas Harvest — Angles and Techniques Worth Noting

### Cold-Open Candidate
**Strength:** The Mars Climate Orbiter crash (m00069 historical note) is ideal for the Attenborough × Feynman style. It is a specific, dated, named-person failure (Bill Clinton's administration, specific mission year 1999, specific cost $325 million) that connects measurement to real consequence.

**Used in rewrite:** Yes, as the primary cold open.

---

### Anchor Points
**Strength:** The source material repeatedly uses reference objects (paper clip = 1 g, baby = 3.5 kg, car = 2 metric tons, liter of water = 1 kg). These are powerful for sense-making.

**Used in rewrite:** Yes, integrated throughout and concentrated in pantry benchmarks.

**Opportunity:** Could be expanded into a sidebar or visual (paper clip next to a gram weight, elephant next to a 5-ton label) for visual learners.

---

### Exponential Insight
**Strength:** The source explicitly teaches why area conversions involve squaring and volume conversions involve cubing. This is the conceptual core that distinguishes metric measurement from memorization.

**Used in rewrite:** Yes, as the organizing principle for Concept 2 (area) and Concept 3 (volume).

**Opportunity:** This could be visualized more dramatically — showing a 1 m square subdivided into 10,000 cm² squares, or a 1 m cube subdivided into 1,000,000 cm³ cubes.

---

### Trade-Off Naming
**Strength:** The source contrasts customary (12, 16, 5,280 factors; optimized for divisibility and human comfort) with metric (powers of 10; optimized for precision and international communication).

**Used in rewrite:** Yes, as an explicit trade-off section in Concept 1.

**Opportunity:** This could be deepened by showing the historical reasons: customary emerged from practical work (inches, hands, feet), metric from Enlightenment rationalism (Earth circumference, water properties).

---

### Reasonableness Judgment
**Strength:** All source modules emphasize judging whether a measurement is reasonable by comparing unit *and* value (not just unit). "5 kilometers for a person's height" is wrong not because km is the wrong unit in general, but because 5 km is absurdly large for a human.

**Used in rewrite:** Yes, integrated into common misconceptions and exercises.

**Opportunity:** This could be formalized as a rubric or checklist: (1) Is the unit appropriate for the object? (2) Is the magnitude plausible given reference objects?

---

### Liter-Cubic-Decimeter Relationship
**Strength:** The liter is the practical unit for liquids, but it is defined as 1 dm³. This connection allows conversion between "everyday" measurements (liters, milliliters) and "geometric" measurements (cm³, m³).

**Used in rewrite:** Yes, emphasized in Concept 3 as the core anchor.

**Opportunity:** A diagram showing a 10×10×10 cm cube holding 1 liter would make this relationship visceral.

---

### Mental-Math Estimation
**Strength:** The source includes approximate temperature conversion formulas ((F − 30)/2 for F→C; 2C + 30 for C→F) that allow reasonable estimates without a calculator.

**Used in rewrite:** Yes, formulas placed in pantry.

**Opportunity:** This could be extended to other conversions: rough estimates for length ("1 meter ≈ 1 yard") and weight ("1 kg ≈ 2.2 lbs" for US-metric bridge).

---

### Multi-Step Problem Complexity
**Strength:** The source includes problems that require multiple unit conversions in sequence (e.g., juice carton: cm³ → mL → L, then count cartons), not just single conversions. These teach dimensional analysis implicitly.

**Used in rewrite:** Yes, in worked examples and challenge exercises.

**Opportunity:** These could be labeled as "dimensional analysis" problems and formalized with the notation $\frac{28,800 \text{ L}}{0.72 \text{ L/carton}} = 40,000 \text{ cartons}$, showing how units cancel.

---

### What the Chapter Does NOT Need
1. **Career spotlights** (Valerie Antoine, whale researcher Fredrik Christiansen) — these distract from the mechanics.
2. **Extended historical context** — brief mention of French Revolution origin is enough; students don't need a full history of metric adoption.
3. **Kelvin scale** — not needed for a general-education chapter on practical measurement.
4. **Hands-on exploration activities** (rolling cylinder experiment, 24-hour metric observation) — these work better as optional enrichment, not core content.
