# Bookmap — Chapter 13 (Math and...)

**Source material:** Six OpenStax source modules covering math applications in art, environment, medicine, music, and sports.

**Purpose:** Extract reusable pedagogical structures, cold opens, worked examples, and ideas for companion essays or chapters.

---

## Source 1: m00038 (Overview)

**Content:** Brief introduction to five application areas (art, environment, medicine, music, sports).

**What it teaches:** The reader understands that mathematics has broad, diverse applications.

**Pedagogical structure:**
- Lists areas without deep explanation
- Promises to "explore a sampling" without pre-announcing themes
- Does not yet explain *why* math appears in these areas

**Cold-open candidate:** None; this is a transition piece.

**Ideas harvest:**
- The overview is weak because it announces what's coming without hooking the reader. A better opening would pick *one* phenomenon (spiral in a sunflower, Bach fugue, Parthenon) and puzzle over it, leaving the reader wondering how math explains it.
- The five-part structure (art, environment, medicine, music, sports) is perhaps too broad for a closing chapter. Picking three deepest concepts and deferring the others to a pantry or essay is a better strategy.

## Source 2: m00112 (Math and Art — Golden Ratio and Fibonacci)

**Content:** Golden ratio definition, Fibonacci sequence, their relationship, golden rectangles, M.C. Escher sidebar.

**What it teaches:**
- $\phi = \frac{1 + \sqrt{5}}{2} \approx 1.618$
- Fibonacci sequence: $F_n = F_{n-1} + F_{n-2}$, starting 1, 1
- Ratio of consecutive Fibonacci terms converges to $\phi$
- Golden rectangle: length-to-width ratio is $\phi$
- Golden rectangles self-replicate (cut off a square, remainder is golden)
- Golden ratio appears in Parthenon, da Vinci, Renaissance paintings, human anatomy

**Pedagogical strengths:**
- Concrete examples (rose petals, daisy, Parthenon, da Vinci)
- Worked examples with actual calculations
- Graded difficulty: definition → sequence → relationship → application

**Pedagogical weaknesses:**
- Does not explain *why* Fibonacci appears in nature (missing the constraint-solution story)
- Treats the relationship between Fibonacci and phi as a mathematical curiosity rather than a consequence of growth under constraints
- The Escher sidebar is interesting but tangential

**Cold-open candidates:**
- Open with a photograph of a sunflower seed head and ask: why do these spirals count out as 21 and 34?
- Open with the Parthenon and ask: why does a building built 2,500 years ago have the same proportions that appear in your face?

**Worked examples worth keeping:**
- Using golden ratio to find navel-floor distance given height
- Computing ratio of consecutive Fibonacci terms
- Determining if a rectangle is golden (ratio test)

**Ideas for essays or extensions:**
- Why does evolution favor the golden ratio in human anatomy? (Biomechanics essay)
- How did Renaissance artists discover the golden ratio? (History and practice)
- Fibonacci in financial markets (Skepticism; why traders believe in Fibonacci retracements)
- Golden ratio in product design (Apple, credit cards, book dimensions)

## Source 3: m00113 (Math and Environment)

**Content:** Water conservation at individual, town, and state scales; solar energy history; power calculations (kilowatts); solar advantages and CO2 emissions.

**What it teaches:**
- Scaling calculations (multiplying individual savings by population and time)
- Unit analysis and dimensional reasoning
- Kilowatt-hour calculations and daily energy budgeting
- Solar panel history and cost reduction
- Greenhouse gas measurement

**Pedagogical strengths:**
- Real-world relevance (climate change, personal energy costs)
- Graded examples: single person → town → state (scale-shift pedagogy)
- Practical calculations (cost of running a refrigerator)

**Pedagogical weaknesses:**
- Disconnected from the book's themes (more about *application* than *pattern*)
- Environment chapter is important for civics but doesn't illustrate how mathematics *describes* natural patterns (unlike the spiral or the octave)

**What this module could become:**
- A full chapter on "Math and Sustainability" (if the textbook expands)
- An essay: "How to calculate your carbon footprint"
- A project: "Design a home solar system" (with real-world constraints)

**Not used in Chapter 13 because:** The golden ratio, Fibonacci, and harmonic intervals are examples of *pattern that emerges under constraints*. Water conservation and solar power are examples of *applying mathematics to solve problems*. The book's final chapter emphasizes the former; the latter, while valuable, was deferred to the pantry.

## Source 4: m00114 (Math and Medicine)

**Content:** Drug dosages (volume, concentration, weight-based); vaccine development timeline; contact tracing as graph theory.

**What it teaches:**
- Dimensional analysis (dose/concentration = volume)
- Weight-based dosing formulas
- Vaccine approval process (10–15 years typically, faster for Covid-19 with parallel phases)
- Contact tracing graphs (vertices = people, edges = contacts; path lengths)

**Pedagogical strengths:**
- High relevance (everyone takes medication; vaccines are current)
- Clear formulas and worked examples
- Contact tracing is graph theory in action (connects to Chapter 12)

**Pedagogical weaknesses:**
- Dosage calculations are straightforward application; not illuminating about why mathematics *appears* in these systems
- Contact tracing is interesting but is just graph data structures, not a deeper pattern

**Cold-open candidates:**
- A pharmacy nurse calculating a dose: "The doctor orders 6 mg, but the vial says 2 mg/mL. How many milliliters?"

**Not used in Chapter 13 because:** This chapter is about how mathematics *describes* natural and artistic patterns under constraint. Medicine is about *using* mathematics to solve practical problems. Both are valid, but they're different registers.

## Source 5: m00115 (Math and Music)

**Content:** Frequency and pitch definitions; decibel levels; half-steps, whole-steps, octaves; equal-temperament tuning; octave frequency relationships.

**What it teaches:**
- Frequency (Hz), pitch (perceptual)
- Simple integer ratios sound consonant (2:1 for octave, 3:2 for fifth, 5:4 for third)
- Irrational ratios (e.g., $\sqrt{2} : 1$ for tritone) sound dissonant
- Equal temperament: each half-step multiplies frequency by $2^{1/12}$
- Octaves are fundamental to all musical traditions
- Frequency relationships are exponential, not linear

**Pedagogical strengths:**
- Universal cultural relevance (music exists everywhere)
- Clear link between mathematics and perception
- Worked examples (computing octave frequencies)
- Explains why equal temperament requires the twelfth root of two
- Historical notes (medieval tritone as "devil's interval")

**Pedagogical weaknesses:**
- Could better explain *why* the $2^{1/12}$ constraint arises from transposability

**Cold-open candidates:**
- A pianist plays two notes; they sound like the same note at different heights. Why? (Octave, 2:1 frequency ratio)
- A clash of two notes (tritone): irrational frequency ratio, brain can't resolve it
- Bach's fugue (from the chapter opening) as example of mathematical structure producing beauty

**Worked examples worth keeping:**
- Computing frequency of note one octave higher
- Identifying intervals by frequency ratios (3:2 for fifth, 4:3 for fourth)
- Understanding why equally-spaced on a keyboard is not equally-spaced in frequency

**Ideas for essays:**
- How did keyboard builders discover equal temperament? (Before calculators, how did they approximate $2^{1/12}$?)
- The mathematics of consonance and dissonance (Why are irrational ratios unpleasant?)
- The cultural universality of the octave (Does every music tradition recognize 2:1?)

## Source 6: m00116 (Math and Sports)

**Content:** Data analytics and team success; round-robin tournament formula $\frac{n(n-1)}{2}$; single-elimination formula $(n-1)$; sports statistics and analysis projects.

**What it teaches:**
- Tournament structures have different mathematical properties
- Number of games grows quadratically for round-robin, linearly for elimination
- Data analytics is now essential in professional sports
- Statistics and analysis tools for evaluating players

**Pedagogical strengths:**
- Engaging topic (students know sports)
- Clear comparison of two tournament models
- Illustrates how constraints (schedule, budget, fairness) lead to different designs

**Pedagogical weaknesses:**
- The tournament formulas are algebraic; they don't illustrate deep pattern (unlike the spiral or the octave)
- Data analytics section is somewhat generic

**Cold-open candidates:**
- A sports manager choosing a tournament format for 20 teams: round-robin means 190 games (impractical), elimination means 19 (feasible but unfair). What's the trade-off?

**Not used in Chapter 13 because:** While this is a valid application, it doesn't fit the chapter's theme of mathematics as the *language of patterns that emerge under constraint*. Sports analytics is mathematics applied to a decision problem, not a description of a natural pattern.

## Structural patterns across sources

### The constraint → solution pattern

**Golden ratio:** Constraint: divide a line such that whole:long = long:short. Solution: $\phi$ emerges.

**Fibonacci and sunflowers:** Constraint: pack seeds in a spiral with maximum density and no waste. Solution: Fibonacci spirals with consecutive-Fibonacci-number ratios emerge.

**Music octaves and equal temperament:** Constraint: transpose melodies and keep them sounding identical. Solution: $2^{1/12}$ spacing emerges.

**Architecture:** Constraint: design a structure that feels balanced and is structurally sound. Solution: $\phi$ proportions emerge (whether intentionally or by intuition).

This pattern — constraint first, then solution, then surprise recognition that the solution is describable by mathematics — is the narrative spine of the chapter. Sources that follow this pattern (golden ratio, Fibonacci, music, octaves) are central. Sources that are mostly "apply the formula" (drug dosages, tournament counting) are supplementary.

### Scale-shift pattern

The chapter uses scale shifts to make mathematics *visible*:
- Individual sunflower seed → sunflower head → human body → Milky Way (all spirals)
- Individual note → octave → musical piece → across all cultures (all use octaves)
- Single proportion → building facade → Renaissance painting → human anatomy (all golden ratio)

Source 3 (environment) uses scale shifts well: one person's water savings → town → state. But the insight is about *multiplication*, not about discovering a pattern at every scale.

## Reusable scaffolding from sources

### Definition-with-precision pattern

From the sources, when a mathematical term is introduced:

**Weak:** "The golden ratio is also known as the golden proportion."

**Strong:** "The golden ratio, denoted $\phi$ (phi), is the unique number such that when you divide a line into two pieces, the ratio of the whole line to the longer piece equals the ratio of the longer piece to the shorter piece. Its value is $\phi = \frac{1 + \sqrt{5}}{2} \approx 1.618$."

The sources do this well for golden ratio, Fibonacci, frequency, octaves. The chapter adopted this pattern.

### Worked example pattern

The sources provide good scaffolding:

1. **State the given information clearly**
2. **Label what you're asked to find**
3. **Show each step** with both symbolic and numerical work
4. **Check against intuition** (does the answer make sense?)
5. **State the general lesson** (what does this example teach about the concept?)

The chapter's worked examples follow this structure.

## Quality of source material

**Strengths:**
- Concrete, grounded examples (real flowers, real paintings, real buildings)
- Worked calculations that can be followed
- Appropriate level for a liberal-arts student

**Weaknesses:**
- Does not always explain the *why* behind patterns
- Some sections feel like lists of applications rather than coherent narratives
- Missing the "constraint → solution → surprise" narrative arc that makes mathematics revelatory
- The five-part structure (art, environment, medicine, music, sports) is sprawling

**Recommendations for revision:**
- Focus on the three concepts that best illustrate "mathematics describes patterns under constraint": art (golden ratio, Fibonacci), music (harmony, octaves), architecture (proportion).
- For each concept, open with a cold-open puzzle, unfold the mathematics, then show the scope (where else does this pattern appear?).
- Defer environment, medicine, and sports to pantry or essays; they are valid but different in kind (applications rather than discoveries of pattern).
- Use the constraint → solution → surprise narrative structure more explicitly.

---

## Overall assessment

The source material is strong on examples and calculations. It would benefit from a more coherent narrative spine. The chapter successfully reorganizes the content around the theme of "mathematics as the language of patterns that emerge under constraint," which gives the closing chapter intellectual weight and answers the deep question: why does mathematics appear in the world?
