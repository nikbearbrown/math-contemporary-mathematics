# Bookmap: Chapter 10 — Geometry Source Analysis

**Source:** OpenStax *Contemporary Mathematics* Chapter 10: Geometry  
**Date:** 2026-05-07  
**Scope:** Nine source modules (m00036, m00125–m00132); three concepts selected and integrated

---

## Source Material Overview

### Module m00036 — Historical Introduction

**Content:** Historical narrative on the role of geometry in ancient Greek mathematics, particularly Euclid and *The Elements*. Frames geometry as a formal axiomatic system. Mentions the Greek prohibition on rulers and the use of only compass and straightedge.

**Reuse:** Cited in cold open and Concept 1 as historical grounding. The insight about Euclid's primitives (point, line) appears in the chapter's treatment of undefined terms.

**Not Used:** The technical discussion of formal axioms and the compass-straightedge construction constraint; outside the scope of a liberal-arts chapter.

---

### Module m00125 — Points, Lines, and Planes

**Content:**
- Definitions: point, line, line segment, ray, plane
- Notation: $\overline{AB}$, $\overrightarrow{AB}$, $\overleftrightarrow{AB}$
- Parallel lines and perpendicular lines with formal definitions
- Union and intersection of sets applied to geometric objects
- Cartesian coordinate planes
- Properties of planes and their intersections

**Reuse:** Core content for Concept 1. All definitions, notation, and the worked example about parallel/perpendicular lines are drawn from this module. The set-theoretic treatment of union and intersection is acknowledged but simplified for the gen-ed audience (deferred to more formal treatment).

**Trade-off:** The chapter treats plane geometry exclusively (2D). The module's 3D plane material (intersecting planes, 3D coordinates) is deferred to a future volume or chapter.

---

### Module m00126 — Angles

**Content:**
- Angle classification by measure (acute, right, obtuse, straight, reflex)
- Angle notation ($\angle ABC$, $m\angle ABC$)
- Protractor use and measurement
- Adjacent angles, supplementary angles, complementary angles
- Vertical angles (opposite angles at intersection are equal)
- Transversals crossing parallel lines: alternate interior angles, alternate exterior angles, corresponding angles
- Properties of parallel lines cut by a transversal

**Reuse:** Definitions and terminology for complementary, supplementary, and vertical angles in Concept 1. The worked example on supplementary angles comes directly from this module. The material on transversals and parallel lines provides the foundation for why parallel lines behave the way they do, though the chapter does not detail all eight angle relationships.

**Trade-off:** The chapter simplifies to the core relationships (corresponding angles, vertical angles, supplementary) and defers the full transversal taxonomy (alternate interior vs. exterior) to the source material.

---

### Module m00127 — Triangles

**Content:**
- Triangle classification by angle (acute, right, obtuse)
- Triangle classification by side (equilateral, isosceles, scalene)
- Sum of interior angles = 180°
- Congruence (SSS, SAS, ASA, AAS theorems)
- Similarity of triangles and scaling factors
- Similar triangles for estimating heights and distances (Thales' method)
- Worked examples: similar triangles, tree height via shadow method

**Reuse:** The sum of interior angles (180°) appears in Concept 1. The insight about similar triangles and proportional sides is central to Concept 3's synthesis section. The worked example of estimating a tree's height via proportional shadows is referenced.

**Trade-off:** Congruence theorems (SSS, SAS, ASA, AAS) are deferred as beyond the scope of a gen-ed geometry chapter. The chapter focuses on similarity and scaling rather than formal congruence proofs.

---

### Module m00128 — Polygons and Perimeter

**Content:**
- Polygon classification by number of sides
- Regular vs. irregular polygons
- Perimeter of rectangles: $P = 2l + 2w$
- Perimeter of regular polygons: $P = n \times s$
- Sum of interior angles: $S = (n - 2) \times 180°$
- Measure of each interior angle: $a = \frac{(n - 2) \times 180°}{n}$
- Exterior angle measure: $b = \frac{360°}{n}$
- Circumference of circles: $C = \pi d$ or $C = 2\pi r$
- Historical note on Archimedes and the approximation of π

**Reuse:** All perimeter formulas and polygon classification appear in Concept 2. The interior angle formula is used in the chapter's integration example (though simplified). Circumference formula is included. The Archimedes note on π is referenced in the pantry file.

**Trade-off:** Exterior angle calculation is deferred; the chapter focuses on interior angles and their sum. The detailed discussion of the Platonic solids and their symmetries is deferred.

---

### Module m00129 — Tessellations

**Content:**
- Transformational geometry: translations, rotations, reflections, glide reflections
- Tessellation properties: no gaps, no overlaps, shapes fit perfectly
- Vertex condition: interior angles at any vertex sum to 360°
- Which regular polygons tessellate (triangle, square, hexagon)
- Transformations used to create tessellations

**Status:** **Deferred.** Tessellations are a beautiful and important topic but require extended treatment beyond the scope of the three-concept chapter. The module is preserved in the source; a future expansion could develop a fourth concept around rigid motions and symmetry.

**Why deferred:** Tessellations are visual and conceptually rich but not as foundational as lines, angles, polygons, area, and the Pythagorean theorem for a gen-ed student. They can be revisited in a dedicated chapter or as a capstone project.

---

### Module m00130 — Area

**Content:**
- Area notation and units (square feet, square meters, etc.)
- Area of triangles: $A = \frac{1}{2}bh$
- Area of rectangles: $A = l \times w$
- Area of parallelograms: $A = b \times h$
- Area of trapezoids: $A = \frac{1}{2}h(a + b)$
- Area of rhombi (diamonds): $A = \frac{d_1 \times d_2}{2}$ (diagonals)
- Area of circles: $A = \pi r^2$
- Worked examples: calculating area for real-world applications (floor tile, city park, kite dimensions)

**Reuse:** All major area formulas and the worked example of calculating floor tile cost appear in Concept 2. The distinction between linear and square units is emphasized.

**Trade-off:** The area of rhombi (using diagonals) is omitted as beyond scope. The chapter focuses on the most common shapes (triangles, rectangles, circles).

---

### Module m00131 — Surface Area and Volume

**Content:**
- Three-dimensional objects: right prisms, cylinders
- Surface area formulas: $SA = 2B + ph$ (prisms), $SA = 2\pi r^2 + 2\pi rh$ (cylinders)
- Volume formulas: $V = B \times h$ (prisms), $V = \pi r^2 h$ (cylinders)
- Real-world applications: greenhouse calculations, apple pie filling cans

**Status:** **Deferred.** Volume and surface area are important but constitute a separate major topic. The module is preserved for future development.

**Why deferred:** Volume requires extension into 3D geometry, which is a significant conceptual leap from 2D plane geometry. A gen-ed chapter on geometry must choose its depth. The three core concepts (angles, perimeter/area, Pythagorean theorem) form a tighter narrative arc.

---

### Module m00132 — Right Triangles and the Pythagorean Theorem

**Content:**
- Pythagorean theorem: $a^2 + b^2 = c^2$
- Pythagorean triples: $(3, 4, 5)$, $(5, 12, 13)$, $(8, 15, 17)$, etc.
- Distance formula derived from Pythagorean theorem: $d = \sqrt{(x_2 - x_1)^2 + (y_2 - y_1)^2}$
- Applications: ladder height, ramp accessibility code, shadow measurements
- Special right triangles: 30°-60°-90° triangle, 45°-45°-90° triangle
- Trigonometric ratios (sine, cosine, tangent) and applications

**Reuse:** The Pythagorean theorem, Pythagorean triples, and real-world applications form the entirety of Concept 3. The cold open scenario (surveyor and pond) is directly inspired by the module's practical applications.

**Trade-off:** Special right triangles (30°-60°-90° and 45°-45°-90°) and trigonometric ratios are deferred. These are more specialized tools that extend beyond the scope of a three-concept chapter. They are noted in the "deferred" section for future development.

## Concepts Chosen and Why

### Concept 1: Lines, Rays, and Angles

**Rationale:** Angles are the foundational language of geometry. Every subsequent concept requires understanding angles. The chapter needs clear terminology and notation before moving to compound shapes.

**Source:** Modules m00125, m00126

**Integration:** Parallel and perpendicular lines (m00125) are introduced as applications of angle relationships. Supplementary angles (m00125, m00126) provide the first worked example.

---

### Concept 2: Polygons, Perimeter, and Area

**Rationale:** Perimeter and area are the most practically useful geometric concepts. They directly apply to real-world measurement: buying materials, calculating costs, planning spaces.

**Source:** Modules m00128, m00130

**Integration:** The worked example of floor tile cost combines both modules' insights: you need to know polygon dimensions (module m00128), compute area (module m00130), and then calculate practical cost.

---

### Concept 3: The Pythagorean Theorem

**Rationale:** The Pythagorean theorem is the most famous mathematical relationship in the Western tradition. It bridges algebra (solving for unknown sides) and geometry (right triangle properties). It appears in countless applications and is a capstone result.

**Source:** Module m00132

**Integration:** The surveyor scenario (cold open) is a real-world application of the Pythagorean theorem. The relationship to similar triangles (m00127) is touched on in the synthesis section.

### Structural Insights from Source

- **Euclid's approach** (m00036): Building geometry on undefined primitives (point, line) and then layering definitions on top is pedagogically sound. The chapter follows this approach.
- **Notation matters** (m00125, m00126): Clear, consistent notation ($\overline{AB}$, $\angle ABC$, etc.) is essential. The pantry file captures all necessary notation.
- **Applications ground abstraction** (m00127, m00130, m00132): Every major concept in the source modules includes real-world examples. The chapter follows this pattern with the surveyor, kitchen, and pond scenarios.
- **Scaling and proportionality** (m00127, m00132): Similar triangles and Pythagorean triples show that scaling is a unifying principle in geometry. This could be made more explicit in a future revision.

## What the Source Does Well

1. **Practical grounding:** Every concept is paired with a tangible example (floor plans, property surveys, shadows, ladder angles). The chapter inherits this strength.

2. **Notation discipline:** The source is careful to introduce and use notation consistently. The pantry file and chapter both honor this.

3. **Historical context:** References to Euclid, Pythagoras, Archimedes, and Thales provide cultural and intellectual anchoring. The chapter includes these.

4. **Progression:** The source moves from points and lines to angles to polygons to area to volume—a logical hierarchy. The chapter compresses this into three concepts but preserves the hierarchy.

---

## What the Source Does Less Well

1. **Unnecessary abstraction:** For a gen-ed audience, the formal set-theoretic treatment of union and intersection of geometric objects (m00125) may be overcomplicated. The chapter simplifies.

2. **Deferred closure:** The source modules end with many advanced topics (tessellations, trigonometry, congruence proofs) that are not developed fully. This is by design—the source is a comprehensive textbook. The chapter makes deliberate choices about scope.

3. **Limited integration:** The source modules largely stand alone. The chapter works to integrate concepts (e.g., showing how angles, perimeter, area, and Pythagorean theorem relate to each other in a surveyor's toolkit).
