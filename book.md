# Contemporary Mathematics

## Title
Contemporary Mathematics — A Liberal-Arts Survey

## Subtitle
Sets, logic, numbers, money, probability, statistics, geometry, voting, graphs — the math you actually use as a citizen, a consumer, and a curious person.

## Author
Nik Bear Brown.

## Audience

This book is written for the gen-ed student taking one math course in college and not majoring in math. They probably last touched serious math in high school. They came in skeptical that math has anything to say to them. The textbook's job is to convince them, through worked examples grounded in things they actually touch, that math is the most useful liberal art.

Specifically:

- Liberal-arts majors completing a quantitative-reasoning requirement.
- Students in nursing, education, social work, business, communications, criminal justice — programs where the math is a service course, not a major requirement.
- Adult learners returning to college.
- Anyone who said "I'm bad at math" in high school and would like to revise that claim.

This is not a course for math majors. It is not a watered-down college algebra. It is mathematics chosen for its civic and personal usefulness — the math that helps you read a credit card statement, evaluate a political poll, make sense of a ranked-choice election, follow a graph theorist's argument about why your route home is shortest.

## Scope

The thirteen chapters cover, in order:

1. **Sets and Venn diagrams** — the basic vocabulary of grouping things and counting their relationships.
2. **Logic** — propositions, truth tables, conditionals, and the kinds of arguments that hold together vs the ones that fall apart.
3. **Real numbers and number theory** — what numbers are, prime factorization, irrationals, the rules of arithmetic.
4. **Number representation and calculation** — place value, other bases, ancient and modern numeration.
5. **Algebra** — equations, inequalities, functions, graphs.
6. **Money management** — percent, interest, loans, mortgages, retirement, taxes, budgeting.
7. **Probability** — counting principles, independent and dependent events, expected value.
8. **Statistics** — data collection, distributions, measures of center and spread, normal distribution, regression.
9. **Metric measurement** — units, conversion, dimensional analysis.
10. **Geometry** — points, lines, polygons, area, volume, similarity, the Pythagorean theorem.
11. **Voting and apportionment** — methods of voting, fairness criteria, Arrow's theorem, apportionment paradoxes.
12. **Graph theory** — vertices and edges, Euler and Hamilton paths, networks, the traveling salesman problem.
13. **Math and** — math and art, math and music, math and architecture, math and the everyday.

Out of scope: derivatives, integrals, formal proof technique, abstract algebra. Those belong in the courses students will take if they decide they want more.

## Voice notes for this book

The Attenborough × Feynman v1.1 voice applies, with a few adjustments for the liberal-arts audience.

**Cold opens earn their place.** A chapter on sets opens in a kitchen drawer, a hospital blood bank, a subway map. Not every subsection needs a fresh scene — that becomes precious. Open the chapter in a scene. Open the major concept sections in a scene. Let the rest of the prose work.

**Every example is anchored.** Math/physics in this book always has a grounded example. Not "let A = {1, 2, 3}." Instead: "Let A be the set of dishes you actually own, and let B be the set of dishes you own that are clean right now." When the source uses an abstract example, replace it with a concrete one and check that the math comes out the same. If the math depends on the abstraction (true for some set-theoretic statements), keep the abstraction but immediately translate it to a concrete instance.

**Notation is taught, not assumed.** This audience has been away from math notation. Every symbol gets a plain-English gloss the first time it appears. The intersection sign $\cap$ is "the things both sets have in common." The complement prime $A'$ is "everything in the universe of discussion that isn't in A." Notation is shorthand earned by the student's understanding, not a hurdle.

**Math density over word count.** A chapter on probability that contains the right worked examples and earned formulas is doing its job at 4,000 words. Word count is a guideline, not a target. If the source supports 8,000 words of substantive material, write 8,000. If 5,000, write 5,000. Never pad.

**Civic stakes are real.** Voting and apportionment chapters carry the most direct civic weight. Money management is the second-most. Both should not flinch from naming what's at stake — gerrymandering, predatory lending, the way compound interest compounds in both directions. The math has consequences for the reader's life and democracy. Say so when it does.

## Prerequisites assumed

- Arithmetic with whole numbers, fractions, and decimals.
- Basic algebra: solving for $x$ in a linear equation, plugging numbers into formulas.
- Comfort reading a coordinate plane.
- Willingness to think.

That's it.
